Product Vision

Celstate

The living-asset layer for software. We give AI assistants the tools to make the digital world feel alive — the way games always have.

Last updated: July 1, 2026

The Problem: Software Forgot How to Feel Alive

I grew up playing Japanese RPGs. Even on old hardware — pixelated, low-resolution, technologically primitive — those worlds felt alive. Trees swayed in the wind. Loot shimmered as you walked past so you wouldn't miss it. A chest slammed shut with visual weight. Two swords clashing carried impact. The atmosphere shifted as you moved through it. None of it was the focal point. The aliveness was in the details around the focal point — the ambient periphery that made the world feel like a world, not a menu.

Everything outside of gaming lost that magic. Web development settled into a vocabulary of stale components — flat cards, static headers, lifeless transitions — not because they're engaging but because they're easy to make work. "Best practices" optimized for reliability, not for delight. The result is a digital landscape where every app, every presentation, every page feels like a form to fill out. Functional. Dead.

Games proved that even low-fidelity technology can create worlds that feel alive. We have vastly more powerful tools now. Why does everything else feel so stale?

The Vision: Alive Software, Composed by AI

Celstate is the living-asset layer for software. We use AI image and video generation models to create the ambient, atmospheric, reactive details that make digital surfaces feel alive — and we expose those capabilities as tools for AI assistants to wield on behalf of their humans.

The protagonist is the AI assistant. No one is writing software by hand anymore — AI writes all software on behalf of creators. Celstate is built for that world. Assistants call our tools to enrich the apps, presentations, and pages they're composing. They generate the transparent ornament that softens a header. The animated element that gives a click its weight. The ambient detail that makes a page feel like a place, not a document.

"Alive" is not the focal point. It's the tree swaying in the background. The shimmer that catches your eye. The impact when something happens. It's the periphery, the atmosphere, the detail that you feel before you notice — the difference between a world you're inside of and a page you scan and leave.

Why It's Hard (And Why That's the Moat)

Making software feel alive requires runtime motion and reactivity — layers that breathe, respond, and change as the user moves through them. This is not a prompt-engineering problem. It's a deep systems problem at the intersection of generative AI, real-time rendering, and software architecture.

We spent a year trying to get there and failed. Transparent video alpha extraction produced ghosting and fringe artifacts. Full-screen generated UI broke layout and accessibility. Runtime motion bundles were too heavy, too brittle, too disconnected from the code that owns the page. Every path we tried — and we tried many — hit the same wall: generated visual richness and deterministic software composition don't naturally compose.

That failure is the moat. If alive software were easy, it would already be everywhere. The problem is deep, defensible, and worth solving. The question is not whether but how — and how to fund the deep research and engineering needed to get there.

The Strategy: Two Horizons

The vision requires motion and reactivity we haven't cracked yet. Rather than starve on the moonshot, we ship what works today to build the runway that funds the deep research. Two horizons, one trajectory.

Horizon 1 — The Runway Engine

Ship alpha-native visual craft that works today

Transparent images, illustrated ornaments, vector animations — assets with real alpha channels that AI assistants can drop into real software without breaking layout. Code owns the page; Celstate owns the transparent art layers that make it feel handcrafted instead of sterile.

This is not a compromise. It's the disciplined beachhead: prove that AI-generated visual craft can elevate real software, build a user base, generate revenue, and learn what creators and assistants actually need.

Horizon 2 — The Moonshot

Genuinely alive software

The deep research: runtime motion, ambient reactivity, atmospheric layers that respond to presence and time. The JRPG feeling — a world that shifts around you as you move through it. Generated visual richness that composes cleanly with deterministic software architecture.

We don't know exactly how to get there yet. We know it requires sustained research beyond prompt engineering — likely combining segmentation/matting priors, temporal stabilization, render QA, and a composition layer that doesn't exist today. Horizon 1 funds the runway to figure it out.

Immediate Priorities

Prioritized by impact and dependency. Earlier items multiply the value of later ones — every failed first generation is a user who never sees the rest of the product. The ordering is not aspirational; it is the sequence that maximizes learning per unit of effort.

1. Prompt Interpretation Layer

The multiplier: Every acquisition channel — SEO, cold-start outreach, agent distribution — routes users into their first generation. If that generation fails, the user leaves and never returns. Even a modest failure rate silently degrades every downstream channel — a hypothetical 60% first-gen success rate would halve the value of all acquisition work. This is the highest-ROI growth feature we have.

  • AI-native prompt massaging before Gemini — an AI layer between the user's input and the Gemini image generation call that rewrites prompts to be compatible with our difference-matting technique. Observed failure: a user asked for an image "with no color" and our black/white difference matting failed. It succeeded when the prompt was "black and white pencil drawing." Instead of building brittle, hard-coded orchestration rules, throw more AI compute at the problem: let an AI model figure out the best prompt phrasing for any given user request.
  • Subsumes the border-transparency fix — when the generated subject fills the canvas edge-to-edge, borderTransparencyRatio drops below the 0.90 threshold even though the matte is clean. This is the most common QA failure mode for portrait/character prompts. If the interpretation layer adds framing qualifiers, the border issue may resolve itself. One observed case: a first-time user's generation ("Emo girl with smile face drawing no color") failed on this false positive and they did not retry — but this is one failure mode among many successful generations, not the typical experience.

2. Sprint-Zero Plumbing

Blocking, not optional. Without attribution and marketing-to-app handoff, every acquisition channel produces uninterpretable traffic. The kill/continue question is literally unanswerable without these.

  • Sitemap — no sitemap exists. Search engines cannot discover and index programmatic landing pages without one. Required before SEO Wave 1 ships.
  • Seeded-prompt URL handoff — ?prompt=&subject=&source= query parameters that deep-link from landing pages into the app with a pre-seeded prompt. Without this, landing-page CTAs land in a blank product state and every visitor starts from zero.
  • "How did you hear about us?" survey — one question on signup. Without it, we cannot attribute signups to channels or distinguish which acquisition efforts are working.

3. Cold-Start Playbook

SEO takes quarters on a greenfield domain. The only channel that produces users this month is human-scale: direct contact with real practitioners. The goal is 10–20 real users we can watch, not traffic.

  • Community participation — 2–3 communities (r/graphic_design, POD/Etsy seller communities, indie game dev Discords). Value-first: share process and proof (native transparency), not link dumps. Conversations, not broadcasts.
  • Direct outreach — 20–50 practitioners (indie brands, small shops, streamers) with a specific ask: try one workflow, 15-min feedback call.
  • Launch / listing surfaces — Product Hunt, relevant directories, "awesome" lists — one at a time with prepared proof.
  • Every outbound link uses source + UTM so attribution stays honest.

4. SEO Wave 1 (Compounding Bet)

A full acquisition strategy exists in docs/strategy/customer-acquisition/.

  • Hub + 8–12 spoke pages — hub at /transparent-png-generator with spokes for high-intent subjects (logo, sticker, character, mascot, etc.). Each spoke has tailored copy, proof images, and a pre-seeded prompt CTA that deep-links into the app.
  • Expect nothing for a quarter. SEO on a greenfield domain has latency — indexing, ranking, and authority all take time. Do not build Wave 2 until Google Search Console shows indexing + impressions.

5. Agent Distribution

The genuinely differentiated channel. We are probably the only transparent-asset generator that's MCP-native, and that channel has near-zero competition right now. This tests the vision's central thesis directly: do assistants, when composing software, reach for this tool?

  • "Connect your assistant" onboarding — one-click flow that generates a personal API key and shows copy-paste instructions for Claude, Cursor, and other agentic clients. The MCP server exists but is invisible to users.
  • MCP registry / directory listings — list the MCP server in registries and directories where agents and developers discover tools.
  • llms.txt / GEO — optimize for being recommended by ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude when users ask "best transparent PNG generator." Clear quotable facts, consistent naming. Parallel to SEO, not replacing it.
  • Agent-facing prompt packs — curated prompt templates designed for assistants to use via MCP. "Generate a transparent logo for a veterinary clinic" → assistant calls the tool with the right parameters.

6. Retention & Re-engagement

The bleeding: Most users who sign up successfully generate images — the product works. But they do a few generations and don't come back. There is no re-engagement loop and limited visibility into whether they found the tool valuable enough to return. We are acquiring users but not retaining them, and learning too little from the churn.

Shipped (sufficient for now):

  • Behavior-driven welcome email — 24h after signup, users are classified into 5 scenarios (credits remaining + downloaded, credits remaining + no download, credits exhausted + downloaded, credits exhausted + no download, no credits used) and sent a tailored email with scenario-specific copy and CTA. Download tracking, daily cron, HMAC-token unsubscribe. Scenarios 1–5 shipped. Scenario 4 includes 3 bonus credits as a diagnostic instrument — if users don't return even with free credits, that's a stronger "no value" signal for the kill framework. Scenario 5 sub-segments "never tried" vs "tried and failed" and uses seeded-prompt deep links (?prompt=...&source=...) so the email CTA pre-fills the generation input, removing the blank-state friction that likely caused the bounce. Shipped (scenarios 1–5).
  • Post-generation feedback — thumbs up/down rating after each generation. Shipped.

Deferred until hundreds of users — nurturing an empty database automates nothing:

  • AI nurturing engine — an AI agent that looks at each user's generation history and decides whether and when to send a re-engagement email. Not hardcoded rules — the AI figures out the right message, timing, and audience. Worthwhile at scale; automates nothing at zero.
  • Autonomous social agent — an AI agent that posts on social media, Reddit, forums, and tracks engagement. If we had a full-time growth marketer, they'd do this. But we don't have enough users to learn from the engagement data yet.
  • Email agent for inbound/outbound — an AI agent that handles all inbound email to @celstate.com, triages and drafts replies. Useful at volume; premature at current scale.
  • Generation history with re-prompting — let users see past generations and fork with a tweaked prompt. Reduces repeat-use friction.
  • Credit depletion nudge with context — contextual "you just made something great, here's what else you could do" instead of a generic paywall.

Lower Priority

  • First-time onboarding (deferred) — post-sign-up guided tour of the UI. Not urgent: every signed-up user has successfully generated an image. The UI is simple enough that a hand-holding flow is a polish item, not a blocker. Defer until we have enough users that drop-off at first generation is actually observed.
  • Live generation demo on landing — let prospective users see the product work without signing up. A single constrained demo generation (watermarked, low-res) could dramatically improve landing-to-signup conversion.
  • Public showcase / gallery (opt-in) — let users share generations to a public gallery. Creates social proof, inspiration for new users, and SEO content.
  • One-click social sharing — every generation gets a shareable public URL. "Made with Celstate" watermark. Users share their creations → their network discovers the tool.
  • Referral-for-credits — "invite a friend, you both get 3 credits." Near-zero CAC, leverages existing users. The growth runbook identifies this (GH-006) but it's not built.

The Horizon

What comes after the runway engine is running. These are not next-sprint items — they are the deep research and capability building that Horizon 1 revenue funds.

  • Alpha animation — extending difference matting to video. Transparent animated subjects with real alpha. Heuristic approaches hit ghosting and fringe artifacts; next step is off-the-shelf segmentation/matting priors combined with Celstate refinement and export QA.
  • Lottie render QA — automated frame capture, visual diffing, blank-frame detection, performance budgets. The gap between "valid JSON" and "render-verified motion quality." Likely needs an external worker since Convex actions can't run headless browsers.
  • Illustrated UI ornaments — transparent art layers for declared slots in real applications. Vignettes, scroll-revealed foliage, hand-drawn semantic icons. The bridge from isolated assets to integrated aliveness in real software.
  • Alive software — the destination. Runtime motion, ambient reactivity, atmospheric layers that respond to presence and time. The JRPG feeling. Not started — funded by Horizon 1 revenue. The open problem: generated visual richness that composes cleanly with deterministic software architecture.

How We Decide It Has No Value

Most users who sign up successfully generate images. The product works for the majority. But a subset hit QA false-positives on their first attempt, and even those who succeed tend to do a few generations and not return. The signal is partially confounded: we can't fully distinguish "product unwanted" from "product broken for some users" until the prompt interpretation layer reduces the failure rate. The kill framework must condition on a working product. These gates are pre-registered: the numbers are written down before we look at the data.

Gate Question Falsification Signal
Quality Does the product work? First-gen success rate <85% after the interpretation layer ships → keep fixing, no verdict yet
Pull Do people who got a successful result come back? Among users whose first 1–3 generations succeeded, <10–15% return within 7 days or do a 2nd session — sustained over ~50+ such users → weak demand
Money Will anyone pay? Of users who deplete free credits, ~0% buy a pack across ~50–100 depletions → economic fail (could be pricing, but at literal zero it's value)
Channel Can you reach buyers repeatably? After 90 days of cold-start + SEO Wave 1: no channel produces a growing weekly signup trickle, and GSC shows impressions but no clicks → positioning fail; no impressions → market too thin
Agent thesis Do assistants use it? 6 months of MCP being discoverable with zero organic agent-driven generations → the "assistant as protagonist" wedge isn't real yet

Time-box: two quarters of honest execution — shipping and distributing, not writing more strategy docs — measured against the gates above. If Quality passes but Pull and Money both fail, that's the clean "no value" verdict: people tried a working product and shrugged.

Exogenous kill trigger: if frontier image models (Gemini, OpenAI) ship native alpha-channel output as a first-class API parameter, the difference-matting moat evaporates overnight. This should trigger an immediate pivot review regardless of where the gates stand.

Scope of "abandon": Horizon 1 (the transparent-PNG utility) is the funding vehicle, not the vision. If Horizon 1 fails its gates, the rational move may be to kill the utility business while continuing the Horizon 2 research on a cheaper footing. Those are separate decisions. Conflating them is how a failed beachhead takes down a still-viable moonshot.
